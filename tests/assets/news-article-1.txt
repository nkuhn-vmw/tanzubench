CITY COUNCIL APPROVES MIXED-USE ZONING FOR HARBOR DISTRICT
Vote Clears Way for 200-Unit Residential Tower Amid Resident Debate

RIVERDALE — After three hours of public testimony that stretched past midnight, the Riverdale City Council voted 5-2 Tuesday to rezone a 1.4-acre parcel along Harbor Boulevard from industrial to mixed-use, paving the way for a 12-story development that would bring 200 residential units and 18,000 square feet of ground-floor retail to the city's long-dormant waterfront.

The project, proposed by Coastal Heights Development Group, has been under review since November after the previous owner of the site — a decommissioned cold-storage facility — sold the land for $8.4 million. Council members Elena Ramirez and Paul Dunmore cast the dissenting votes.

"The Harbor District has waited 20 years for this kind of investment," said Council President Janelle Okafor, who championed the rezoning. "This project brings affordable units, street-level retail, and tax revenue that directly funds our schools."

Of the 200 units, 40 — or 20 percent — must be rented at below-market rates as part of a community benefit agreement negotiated with the developer. Coastal Heights also agreed to contribute $350,000 to a nearby park renovation fund and reserve ground-floor space for local businesses at a discounted lease rate for the first five years.

Opponents packed the council chambers. A group called Riverdale Neighbors First circulated a petition signed by more than 1,200 residents who said the tower would block waterfront views, strain the existing sewer infrastructure, and accelerate gentrification already pushing longtime renters out of adjacent neighborhoods.

"Twenty percent affordable isn't enough when the remaining 160 units will rent for $3,200 a month," said Priya Shetty, a tenant advocate who addressed the council during public comment. "We're building for people who are already here and being displaced."

Councilmember Dunmore agreed, arguing that the environmental review had not adequately assessed traffic impacts on Harbor Boulevard, a two-lane road that already backs up during morning rush hour.

"We're being asked to approve a 200-unit tower on a street that can't handle the cars already on it," Dunmore said. "The traffic study relied on pre-pandemic commute data. That's not good enough."

City traffic engineer Marcus Webb defended the analysis, noting that updated counts from 2025 showed average daily volume on Harbor Boulevard had recovered to only 78 percent of 2019 levels. He said intersection improvements funded by the developer would bring the road to acceptable service levels.

Construction is expected to begin in early 2027, pending state environmental clearance and design review approval from the city's planning board. The building is projected to be completed by late 2029.

The Riverdale Neighbors First coalition said it would consult with attorneys about a potential legal challenge.

# Source A: The Productivity Case for Remote Work

Research conducted over the past decade has consistently pointed toward remote work as a net productivity gain for knowledge workers. A landmark study by Stanford economist Nicholas Bloom tracked 500 call center employees over nine months: remote workers completed 13.5 percent more calls than their in-office counterparts, attributing the gains to fewer sick days, shorter breaks, and quieter working conditions.

Similar findings appear across industries. A 2022 survey by the software analytics firm Prodoscore analyzed keystrokes, emails, and application usage across 100,000 knowledge workers and found that employees working from home produced, on average, 5 percent more output per hour than those working on-site. The effect was most pronounced among mid-career workers aged 35 to 50, who cited reduced commute fatigue as the single largest driver.

Proponents also point to the talent acquisition angle. Companies offering remote-first arrangements report 40 percent larger applicant pools for technical roles, according to a 2023 Hired.com labor market report, enabling selectivity that in turn raises team-average output. Remote-first firms surveyed in the same report experienced 18 percent lower voluntary attrition, reducing the productivity drag of onboarding cycles.

Critics who cite collaboration losses often conflate synchronous communication with productive work. Remote workers compensate for fewer hallway conversations through more deliberate asynchronous documentation — wikis, recorded decision logs, shared project trackers — that some researchers argue leaves organizational knowledge more accessible and durable than oral tradition passed across conference tables.

The evidence is not unanimous: highly creative roles that depend on rapid iterative sketching show weaker productivity gains, and junior employees who lack dedicated home workspaces report disproportionate focus challenges. But for the median knowledge worker in a role that does not require physical co-presence, the data tilts clearly toward remote work as a productivity-positive arrangement.
